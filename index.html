Understanding Your Pelvic Floor Risk at Birth

Personalised, evidence-based risk information to help you and your care team make decisions together about mode of birth, prevention, and your care.

📋 About This Tool

This tool helps you understand the risks of pelvic floor injury during childbirth and the likelihood of longer-term pelvic floor conditions, based on your individual circumstances. It is for shared decision-making between you and your care team — it does not replace clinical advice.

📊 What This Tool Shows

  • Rates of serious perineal tears (OASI) and levator muscle injury
  • Longer-term risks: urinary incontinence, anal incontinence, prolapse, sexual difficulties
  • How your individual risk factors affect your risk
  • What can be done to reduce risk — by you and your care team
  • Risks for all modes of birth compared side-by-side

🌱 Key Messages

  • Many women give birth without significant pelvic floor injury
  • Most pelvic floor conditions can be treated effectively
  • Evidence-based prevention strategies exist and work
  • Your preferences and values matter in birth planning
  • This tool is for education and shared decision-making

📈 All Modes of Birth Are Shown

This app does not ask you to pre-select a mode of birth. Risks for all birth modes are shown so you can compare them together with your care team.

  • Spontaneous vaginal birth (SVB)
  • Vacuum (ventouse) — non-rotational
  • Forceps — non-rotational
  • Rotational / mid-cavity assisted birth
  • Emergency caesarean (1st stage)
  • Emergency caesarean (2nd stage)
  • Planned (elective) caesarean

🛡️ Patient Decision Aids

Reducing Your Risk

Evidence-based patient decision aids to help you understand your options, what you can choose, and what your clinical team will consider when helping you give birth as safely as possible.

👶 Big Babies & Timing of Vaginal Birth

When a baby is estimated to be large (≥4 kg or ≥4.5 kg), the timing and planning of birth becomes an important discussion. This aid explains the evidence, the options, and when an elective caesarean might be considered.

✅ What we know: The Big Baby Trial (Lancet 2023)

Induction of labour at 38 weeks for suspected macrosomia (≥4.0 kg) significantly reduced shoulder dystocia (from 7.7% → 3.3%), reduced emergency CS rates, and did not increase instrumental birth. This is now recommended practice when ultrasound suggests a large baby near term.

Pasupathy D et al. Lancet 2023;401:1927-38.

⚠️ Shoulder dystocia: the highest risk factor for OASI

When the baby's shoulder gets stuck (shoulder dystocia), emergency manoeuvres are required — this is associated with an OASI risk of OR ~12.1 (vs birth without shoulder dystocia). EFW ≥4.5 kg increases shoulder dystocia risk approximately 5-fold.

Packet M et al. Ultrasound Obstet Gynecol 2023;62:486–496.

How is the baby's size estimated?

📊 Hadlock formula

The standard ultrasound formula combining biparietal diameter (BPD), head circumference (HC), abdominal circumference (AC), and femur length (FL). It has ±15–20% error at term (e.g. a baby estimated at 4,000 g may actually weigh 3,400–4,600 g).

📈 Customised growth charts

Adjust for maternal height, weight, ethnicity, parity, and sex of baby. These are better at identifying babies who are large for their constitution — avoiding both over-medicalisation of constitutionally large babies and under-detection of growth-restricted babies.

🤚 Clinical palpation

Less accurate than ultrasound for detecting macrosomia (sensitivity ~40%). An ultrasound EFW at 36 weeks is the standard of care when large baby is suspected by clinical assessment or growth chart.

🎗️ Reducing Risk of Anal Sphincter Injury — Perineal Bundle

An OASI (3rd or 4th degree tear) occurs in ~3–5% of first-time vaginal births, and up to 22% with rotational forceps. A bundle of four evidence-based interventions can meaningfully reduce this risk — but each requires your informed agreement.

✅ The EVA Trial (BMJ 2024) — Strongest evidence

A randomised controlled trial of 2,811 women showed that a mediolateral episiotomy at ≥60° from midline in vacuum-assisted birth: halved OASI rate (RR 0.44, 95% CI 0.28–0.68); NNT = 24 (one OASI prevented for every 24 episiotomies performed); no increase in 3rd degree or higher tears.

Bergendahl S et al. BMJ 2024;385:e079014.

✅ Meta-analysis: Bundle components together (Okeahialam 2022)

Perineal bundle (warm pack + hands-on + episiotomy) reduced OASI: vacuum OR 0.51 (49% reduction), forceps OR 0.32 (68% reduction). Applies to all primiparous women having an assisted birth.

Okeahialam NA et al. Int Urogynecol J 2022;33:1393–1405.

The four elements of the perineal bundle

1

🤲 Hands-on perineal support (HOOP)

The midwife or doctor places a hand on the perineum as the baby's head crowns, applying gentle pressure and guiding the birth. Also called "guarding the perineum."

Evidence: OR for OASI = 0.43 (57% reduction). RCT: McCandlish et al.; meta-analysis: Aasheim et al. Cochrane 2017.
Shared: Patient should indicate consent; clinician applies technique
2

🌡️ Warm perineal compress

A warm, moist cloth applied to the perineum as the baby is descending in the second stage. Temperature 38–44°C. May reduce crowning pain and perineal trauma.

Evidence: Cochrane review (Aasheim et al. 2017): significant reduction in 3rd/4th degree tears (RR 0.48, 95% CI 0.28–0.84) and perineal pain at birth.
Patient can request this / accept or decline
3

✂️ Mediolateral episiotomy at ≥60° from midline

A surgical cut to widen the vaginal opening when indicated — not routinely. Must be cut at 60° or more from the midline to be protective. A midline cut (straight back) significantly increases OASI risk.

EVA Trial (BMJ 2024): OASI halved (RR 0.44). RCOG recommends for all assisted vaginal births when clinical features suggest high OASI risk.
Shared: Patient must consent; clinician decides if and when needed
4

🔍 Systematic examination after every birth

Every woman should have a systematic examination immediately after birth using the Sultan classification to detect any tears. This ensures injuries are identified and repaired promptly, reducing long-term complications.

RCOG Green-top Guideline No. 29 (2015): Recommends examination by a trained clinician after every vaginal birth to classify any perineal trauma.
Clinician responsibility — every woman, every birth

🙋 What can I decide?

Patient can decide
  • Request the full perineal bundle be documented in your birth plan
  • Accept or decline the warm compress (evidence-based, low risk)
  • Discuss consent for episiotomy before labour begins — state your preferences
  • Ask your clinician what their episiotomy angle practice is (≥60° is required for protection)
Clinician responsibility
  • Applying hands-on perineal support at crowning
  • Deciding when an episiotomy is clinically indicated (based on crowning angle, tissue stretch, instrument use)
  • Cutting at the correct angle (≥60°) — a 45° cut provides little protection
  • Examining all women systematically after birth for tear classification

⚠️ Mode of Birth After Previous Anal Sphincter Injury

A previous OASI (3rd or 4th degree tear) is one of the most significant factors in planning a subsequent birth. The key questions are: what is the risk of a recurrent tear, and what is the risk of worsening your existing bowel function?

⚠️ Recurrent OASI: what the evidence shows

The overall recurrence rate for OASI in a subsequent vaginal birth is approximately 7–10% in women without symptoms. For women with bowel symptoms (urgency, leakage) after the previous tear, a further vaginal birth risks worsening symptoms in approximately 20–30%.

Fitzpatrick M et al. BJOG 2002;109:289-94; Jangö H et al. BJOG 2012;119:1044-52.

✅ What tests are important before deciding?

Anorectal physiology studies (manometry + endoanal ultrasound or MRI) can identify: (1) whether the sphincter has healed with an intact or deficient repair, (2) whether resting and squeeze pressures are normal. This informs risk of symptom worsening and helps guide mode of birth recommendation.

Sultan AH & Thakar R. Int Urogynecol J 2012;23:1541–5; RCOG GTG 29 2015.

Patient can decide
  • Request full anorectal physiology studies to inform your decision — you are entitled to this before committing to a birth plan
  • Choose planned caesarean section even if your tests are normal and you have no symptoms — this is a valid, supported choice
  • Ask for a specialist referral (urogynaecologist or colorectal surgeon) before making a decision
  • Proceed with a planned vaginal birth after full counselling if asymptomatic with normal tests
Clinician responsibility
  • Arranging anorectal studies where clinically indicated (recommended for all women with previous OASI)
  • Documenting and communicating the degree and grade of the previous tear
  • Applying the perineal bundle and considering prophylactic episiotomy if vaginal birth proceeds
  • Providing referral to relevant specialist prior to delivery if symptoms or abnormal tests

⚡ Decision-Making at Full Dilatation — Method of Birth in the Second Stage

When you reach full dilatation (10 cm), you and your clinical team will assess how birth is progressing. Sometimes birth needs assistance. This aid explains what options are available, the evidence, what you can decide, and what your care team will consider.

📖 Key principle: No birth is risk-free

Unplanned emergency procedures involve complex, real-time decision-making where the clinician must determine the safest mode of birth — the option with the lowest combined maternal and fetal risk — and seek your consent. The most immediate risks (fetal compromise, maternal bleeding) drive these decisions, not only the longer-term pelvic floor risks, which are weighed in your broader birth planning discussions.

Murphy DJ et al. RCOG Assisted Vaginal Birth Green-top Guideline No. 26. BJOG 2020;127.

✅ Favourable: OA position, head well-descended (≥+1 station)

The baby is facing your spine (occiput anterior), the head is low in the pelvis. This is the most common scenario when assistance is needed — most women in this situation can give birth vaginally with skilled help.

Options when push is needed: Vacuum vs Forceps vs CS

💨 Vacuum (Ventouse)

What it does: A suction cup is placed on the baby's head. During contractions and your pushing, gentle traction assists delivery.

OASI risk vs SVB: OR ~2.0–3.0 (doubles-triples risk)

Levator avulsion risk: OR ~1.5–2.0 vs SVB

Success rate (vaginal birth): ~80–85% with primary instrument

Baby risks: Cephalhaematoma (bruising under scalp) ~10%; rarely: subgaleal haemorrhage (<1%)

Advantages: Lower maternal soft tissue injury than forceps; lower OASI risk than forceps; can be performed without epidural

Patient must consent; clinician selects instrument based on assessment
⚙️ Forceps (Non-rotational)

What it does: Two curved metal blades (spoons) cradle the baby's head on each side. Traction + guidance on each push delivers the baby.

OASI risk vs SVB: OR ~3.0–5.0 (Neville-Barnes, Wrigley's at outlet)

Levator avulsion risk: OR ~2.5–3.5 vs SVB (higher than vacuum)

Success rate: ~92–95% — higher than vacuum for primary delivery

Baby risks: Minor facial marks (temporary); very rarely facial nerve palsy (<0.5%)

Advantages: Higher success rate than vacuum; preferred when baby has a bleeding disorder or scalp concerns; preferred by some clinicians at outlet

Patient must consent; clinician selects instrument
🏥 CS at Full Dilatation (2nd stage CS)

What it does: Baby is delivered by abdominal surgery when the cervix is fully dilated and the baby is in the pelvis.

OASI risk: Very low (<1%). Near zero levator avulsion risk.

Maternal risks: Significant. Higher blood loss than 1st stage CS; bladder/bowel injury risk; very challenging technically when baby is deeply engaged — higher maternal morbidity than AVB in many scenarios.

Future pregnancy risks: Uterine rupture scar; placenta praevia/accreta risk increases with each CS.

Baby risks: Generally similar or better than complicated AVB for acute fetal distress.

Patient must consent; clinician assesses feasibility of AVB vs CS

🙋 What can I decide — favourable position?

Patient can decide / request
  • Express preference for vacuum vs forceps — discuss with your clinician during labour
  • Ask about the clinician's experience with each instrument
  • Discuss whether the perineal bundle (warm pack, hands-on, episiotomy) will be used
  • Ask whether a brief trial of pushing (passive descent) is appropriate before an instrument is used
  • Request CS as your preference — but understand the clinical tradeoffs at full dilatation
Clinician determines
  • Whether instrumental birth is indicated (maternal or fetal reason)
  • Selection of instrument based on clinical assessment (position, station, asynclitism)
  • Whether a trial of instruments in theatre is appropriate
  • Whether to abandon trial and proceed to CS if instrument is not succeeding
  • Verbal consent is acceptable in emergency time-critical situations (NSW Health Consent Manual 2025)

⚠️ Unfavourable position or descent: OP or OT at full dilatation

When the baby is in an occiput posterior (OP — facing forward, "back-to-back") or occiput transverse (OT — facing sideways) position at full dilatation, the baby's presenting diameter is larger, descent is slower, and the risk of needing an operative birth and sustaining a tear is significantly higher. OR for OASI ~2.0 compared to OA position.

Options when baby is in OP or OT position

🔄 Manual Rotation

What it does: The clinician's hand is inserted and the baby's head is gently rotated to OA (occiput anterior) position. If successful, a non-rotational instrument or spontaneous birth can then occur.

Success rate: ~70–80%.

If successful — OASI risk: Equivalent to non-rotational AVB (much lower than rotational instruments).

Advantages: Avoids rotational instrument use; lower OASI and neonatal injury risk than Kielland's forceps when successful.

Risks if unsuccessful: Requires conversion to rotational instrument or CS.

Bahl R et al. BJOG 2013;120:1526-33.

Patient can request / must consent; clinician assesses feasibility
🔄 Rotational Vacuum (Kiwi/OmniCup)

What it does: A specially shaped vacuum cup that allows rotation of the baby's head as traction is applied. Requires skill and experience.

OASI risk: OR ~3.5–5.0 vs OA SVB

Sequential instrument risk: Higher than forceps (~37% vs 0.6% with manual rotation + forceps).

Neonatal outcomes: Comparable to rotational forceps for cord pH and Apgar.

Clinician decides based on clinical assessment; patient must consent
⚙️ Kielland's (Rotational) Forceps

What it does: Specialised forceps designed to rotate the baby from OP/OT to OA during delivery. Requires significant skill and is typically performed by a senior obstetrician in a room equipped for CS if needed.

OASI risk: OR ~5.0–8.0 vs OA SVB. Highest OASI risk of all birth methods.

Success rate: ~90% in experienced hands.

Maternal risks: Highest OASI, PPH, and cervical/vaginal laceration risk.

Baby: Comparable cord pH and Apgar to rotational vacuum in experienced hands.

Bahl R et al. BJOG 2013;120:1526-33; RCOG GTG 26 2020.

Senior clinician assessment + patient consent essential
🏥 Emergency CS at Full Dilatation

What it does: Abdominal birth when baby is deeply engaged in the pelvis (OP/OT) — technically very challenging. The baby's head may need to be disimpacted from the pelvis before delivery.

Maternal risk: Highest maternal morbidity of all options when performed at full dilatation with a deeply engaged head (blood loss, bladder injury, uterine extensions).

OASI risk: Very low (<1%).

Future pregnancies: Uterine scar risks with each subsequent CS.

When preferred: Fetal compromise not likely to tolerate further instrument attempts; clinician not experienced with rotational instruments; failed instrument trial.

Clinician determines safest option; patient consents

🙋 What can I decide — OP/OT position?

Patient can decide / request
  • Indicate in your birth plan that you would consider manual rotation — if recorded before labour, this helps your care team act quickly without delay in gaining consent
  • Ask about the clinician's experience with manual rotation and Kielland's forceps
  • Express preference for CS over rotational instruments if you are concerned about OASI risk (discuss tradeoffs — 2nd stage CS carries its own significant risks)
  • Ask whether there is time to allow the baby to rotate passively with position changes (upright, lateral, hands-and-knees)
Clinician determines
  • Assessment of position and station using examination (± ultrasound)
  • Whether to attempt manual rotation, rotational instrument, or proceed to CS
  • Whether a trial in theatre is appropriate (CS available if instrument fails)
  • Supervision level required (Kielland's and mid-cavity procedures require senior obstetrician)
  • Verbal consent is acceptable in time-critical emergency scenarios (NSW Health 2025)

🏥 When emergency caesarean section is indicated

Sometimes vaginal birth — whether spontaneous or assisted — is not possible or safe. Your clinical team will advise an emergency CS when certain situations arise. Understanding these situations in advance helps you be prepared for a rapid discussion and consent.

Indications for emergency CS at full dilatation

🔴 Acute fetal compromise

Abnormal fetal heart rate tracing (CTG) suggesting the baby is not tolerating labour and needs to be delivered urgently. If AVB cannot be achieved rapidly, CS is preferred.

Clinician determines — verbal consent in emergency acceptable

🔴 Failed instrumental trial

If a vacuum or forceps attempt is not progressing — the baby's head is not descending despite good effort — the instrument should be abandoned and CS performed. Continuing beyond a safe trial risks injury to both baby and mother.

Clinician decision; patient informed immediately

🟡 No instrument available or operator not experienced

If the clinical situation requires mid-cavity or rotational forceps, but a suitably experienced operator is not present, CS is safer than an attempted instrument in inexperienced hands.

Clinician assessment; shared discussion if time allows

🟡 Maternal clinical deterioration

Significant bleeding (PPH), maternal exhaustion where further pushing is not possible, or acute maternal illness where the birth needs to be expedited rapidly.

Clinician decision + patient consent

⚠️ 2nd stage CS is not without risk

A caesarean section performed at full dilatation with a deeply engaged fetal head is technically challenging and carries significantly higher maternal morbidity than CS in the first stage — including higher rates of haemorrhage, bladder injury, and uterine extensions. It is also associated with increased complications in future pregnancies (placenta praevia, placenta accreta, uterine rupture). The aggregate risk of CS at full dilatation can exceed that of AVB in experienced hands.

Bahl R et al. Am J Obstet Gynecol 2024;230:S917–S931; Murphy DJ et al. BJOG 2020;127.

🙋 What can I decide about emergency CS?

Patient can express preferences before labour
  • Document in your birth plan whether you have strong preferences about emergency procedures
  • Discuss before labour the circumstances under which you would prefer CS over attempted instruments — this helps your team understand your priorities in an emergency
  • Ask your clinician: "At what point would you recommend stopping an instrument trial and proceeding to CS?"
Clinician determines in real time
  • Category of urgency (Category 1 — immediate threat to life; Category 2 — not immediate threat but needs expediting)
  • Whether to perform a trial of instruments in theatre first
  • Technique to safely disimpact the baby's head during 2nd stage CS
  • Verbal consent is legally and ethically appropriate in genuine time-critical emergencies (NSW Health 2025; Montgomery v Lanarkshire 2015)

Pelvic Floor & Childbirth

Understanding your anatomy, how birth affects the pelvic floor, your risk factors, and evidence-based steps to protect these tissues during labour and birth.

1. Pelvic Floor Anatomy

The pelvic floor is a hammock-shaped group of muscles, ligaments, and connective tissue spanning the base of the pelvis. It supports the bladder, uterus, and rectum; maintains continence; contributes to sexual function; and at birth must stretch to 3–5 times its normal area to allow passage of the baby's head.

Key Structures

  • Levator Ani complex — the main pelvic floor muscle. Includes puborectalis, pubococcygeus, and iliococcygeus. Normal hiatal area ~25 cm²; must expand to 60–80 cm² during birth
  • Perineal body — fibromuscular node between vagina and anus. Short perineal body (<2.5 cm) increases OASI risk significantly
  • Anal sphincter complex — external (voluntary) and internal (involuntary) sphincters. Injury causes OASI (3rd/4th degree tear)
  • Pudendal nerve (S2–S4) — innervates sphincters and perineal skin. Safe stretch ratio only 1.2–1.3× — far exceeded during birth

Sources: Ashton-Miller & DeLancey. Annu Rev Biomed Eng 2009; Svabik et al. BJOG 2009; Lien et al. Obstet Gynecol 2004.

3. Evidence-Based Prevention — The Perineal Bundle

✅ Better Births for Women Bundle (WHA / Safer Care Victoria)

This evidence-based bundle of four interventions can significantly reduce serious perineal tears and levator muscle injury.

  • Hands-on perineal support with gentle verbal guidance at crowning — OR 0.43 (57% reduction in OASI)
  • Warm perineal compress (38–44°C) at onset of stretching and crowning — ~50% reduction in severe crowning pain; possible OASI reduction
  • Mediolateral episiotomy at ≥60° from midline when indicated — EVA Trial (BMJ 2024): OASI halved (RR 0.44, NNT 24); forceps OR 0.32 (68% reduction)
  • Systematic examination of all women post-birth using Sultan classification

Sources: EVA Trial — Bergendahl et al. BMJ 2024;385:e079014; Okeahialam et al. Int Urogynecol J 2022; Dahlen et al. Birth 2007; Räisänen et al.
