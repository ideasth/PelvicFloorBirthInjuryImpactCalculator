Risk Communication

How Risk is Shown in this App

This page explains the methods used to present risk clearly, honestly, and usefully for shared decision-making.

The PANDA Approach

All risk communication in this app follows the PANDA framework (adapted from Gigerenzer & Edwards BMJ 2003; RCOG 2015).

P

Personalised

Risks are adjusted for your age, weight, baby's size, parity, and labour factors — not just population averages.

A

Absolute numbers

"5 in 100 women" is far easier to understand than "5% risk" or "doubled risk." This app always uses natural frequencies with a denominator of 100.

N

Numeracy-supported

A picture of 100 people (icon array / Cates plot) shows exactly who is affected and who is not — substantially improving comprehension for all patients regardless of numeracy (Garcia-Retamero & Galesic 2010).

D

Decision Aid

Risks are shown in context: baseline population risk, your personal risk, and whether a clinical or personal action can reduce that risk.

A

Anchored in everyday language

Each risk band is named (Very Common to Very Rare) with an everyday equivalent — "a person in your family" to "a person in a large town" — per RCOG 2015 standard.

Evidence base for this approach

Icon arrays (Cates plots) substantially improve risk comprehension for low-numeracy patients and reduce framing bias. Absolute frequencies are understood far better than percentages or relative risks. This app avoids relative-risk figures in isolation, always pairing them with the absolute frequency.

Gigerenzer & Edwards, BMJ 2003 | Garcia-Retamero & Galesic, Health Psychol 2010 | Okan et al., Risk Anal 2019 | RCOG, 2015

What is levator ani avulsion?

Tearing of the pubovisceral (pubococcygeus) muscle from its insertion on the pubic bone, detectable by 3D/4D ultrasound or MRI. Associated with POP (OR 4–8), UI, and pelvic pain. Clinical Education

Pelvic Floor Anatomy, Childbirth Injury & Prevention

Evidence-based overview for clinicians and patients — from anatomy through risk factors to the prevention bundle.

🏸

Pelvic Floor Anatomy and Childbirth Mechanics

Supports, muscles, fasciae, nerves

The pelvic floor is a multilayer musculofascial diaphragm spanning the bony pelvis. Its primary components are the levator ani group (pubococcygeus/pubovisceralis, puborectalis, iliococcygeus), the coccygeus, and the external and internal anal sphincters (EAS, IAS). Anterior support is provided by the pubourethral ligaments and arcus tendineus fascia pelvis; middle compartment by the uterosacral–cardinal ligament complex; posterior by the rectovaginal fascia and perineal body.

During vaginal birth, the pelvic floor undergoes extreme stretch. The levator hiatus — the aperture through which the baby descends — must enlarge from ~6 cm² to up to 25–30 cm² (a ~200–300% strain). At 38–39 weeks, the fetal head must negotiate the levator plate at angles that test the structural limits of the pubovisceral muscle. This "capacity–demand" model (DeLancey 2021) explains why the same birth can cause levator avulsion in one woman but not another of similar dimensions.

Key reference: DeLancey capacity–demand model

DeLancey JO. "The pathophysiology of pelvic organ prolapse." Neurourol Urodyn. 2021. The model holds that injury occurs when birth forces exceed the individual woman's structural capacity — determined by muscle cross-sectional area, birefringence, and connective tissue quality. Strategies that reduce demand (IOL timing, mode of birth) or increase capacity (physio) are the levers for prevention.

Pubovisceral muscle (PVM)

Most vulnerable to avulsion injury. Runs from the posterior pubis around the anorectal junction. Avulsion from the pubic bone is the commonest severe birth injury, present in ~15–35% of vaginally delivering nulliparous women.

External anal sphincter (EAS)

Striated voluntary muscle, 2–3 cm in length, forms a toroidal ring around the anal canal. Torn in OASI. EAS disruption causes passive and urge faecal incontinence. Partial injuries (3a) have better prognosis than complete (3c/4).

Internal anal sphincter (IAS)

Smooth involuntary muscle (continuation of rectal circular muscle). Provides 70–80% of resting anal tone. Disruption results in soiling, urgency, and passive loss. Torn in grade 3c and 4 OASI.

Perineal body

Fibromuscular node at the midpoint of the perineum — the convergence of the superficial and deep transverse perineal muscles, EAS, bulbospongiosus and vaginal wall. Disruption in 2nd–4th degree tears reduces perineal body height; key target of repair.

OASI — Risk Factors and Prognosis

3rd and 4th degree perineal tears

Obstetric Anal Sphincter Injuries (OASI) are graded 3a–4 using the Sultan classification (Sultan AH 1994). OASI is the leading cause of faecal incontinence in women under 50. The national rate in Australia is ~3.1% (all births); rates in nulliparous instrumental births reach 28–40% without a prevention bundle.

Long-term consequences of OASI

Faecal incontinence

50–60% of women with OASI have some AI at 12 months. Gas/liquid loss ~40%; solid loss ~10–15%. 3c/4 injuries carry the worst prognosis. Worse with IAS injury, pudendal neuropathy, and infection.

Faecal urgency

Present in ~30–40% at 1 year. Associated with EAS and IAS disruption. May respond to biofeedback and sacral neuromodulation.

Dyspareunia

Perineal pain during intercourse in 20–35% at 6 months, ~10–18% at 1 year. Related to perineal body height reduction and pelvic floor hypertonicity.

Psychological impact

Birth trauma and OASI are associated with PTSD, postnatal depression, and tokophobia in subsequent pregnancy. Shared decision-making and debrief reduce impact.

Levator Ani Avulsion

Muscle detachment from pubic bone

Levator ani avulsion (LAA) is defined as disruption of the attachment of the pubovisceral (pubococcygeus) muscle from the internal surface of the inferior pubic ramus, detectable on 3D/4D transperineal ultrasound or MRI. It is not visible on 2D imaging. Prevalence: 13–35% of vaginally delivering women; up to 40% after forceps birth. LAA is the most powerful modifiable structural predictor of pelvic organ prolapse (OR 4–8 for symptomatic prolapse). It cannot be surgically repaired and does not spontaneously heal.

Clinical significance

Levator avulsion should be considered in any woman presenting with new pelvic organ prolapse, especially when forceps birth was part of her obstetric history. Diagnosis requires 3D/4D TPUS or MRI. Women with avulsion benefit from supervised pelvic floor physiotherapy, pessary management for prolapse, and consideration of reconstructive surgical planning when family complete.

🌟

Long-Term Pelvic Floor Dysfunction

UI, AI, POP, Sexual Dysfunction

Pelvic floor dysfunction (PFD) encompasses urinary incontinence (UI), anal incontinence (AI), pelvic organ prolapse (POP), and sexual dysfunction. PFD affects approximately 1 in 3 women over their lifetime, with childbirth being the single most important modifiable risk factor. The EPINCONT study (Wesnes et al.) and the Swedish twin study (Gyhagen et al. BJOG 2013) provide the strongest longitudinal evidence.

Urinary incontinence (UI)

Affects 15–35% of women after vaginal birth (stress, urge, or mixed). C/S does not eliminate UI risk (8–15%). Physio reduces risk 30–50% if started antenatally. Key predictor: levator avulsion and OASI history. Gyhagen et al. BJOG 2013; Wesnes 2021.

Anal incontinence (AI)

Gas, liquid, or solid stool leakage. 20–30% of women with OASI have AI at 10 years; worse for 3b–4 injuries and IAS disruption. Significantly higher after forceps (30–50% AI) vs SVB (12–22%). Daly JO UGSA 2023.

Pelvic organ prolapse (POP)

Symptomatic prolapse (POPQ ≥2): 8–15% after SVB, up to 50% after SVB+levator avulsion. Highly associated with forceps (OR 3–5). Lifetime risk of surgical correction ~13%. Gyhagen et al. BJOG 2013.

Sexual dysfunction

Dyspareunia present in 10–35% at 6 months post-birth (vaginal). Lower sexual satisfaction in 25–40% at 1 year after OASI. C/S: lower short-term dyspareunia but equivalent satisfaction at 1 year. Daly JO UGSA 2023.

UR-CHOICE model

The UR-CHOICE model (Chan et al. AJOG 2014) — available at riskcalc.org/UR_CHOICE/ — provides individualised 5-year estimates for urinary incontinence, prolapse, and AI based on age, BMI, mode of birth, parity, and OASI history. This dashboard's calculator uses the UR-CHOICE base rates, adjusted with published ORs for local factors.

Prevention Bundle

Evidence-based interventions to reduce OASI

A comprehensive prevention bundle combining hands-on perineal support, warm perineal packing, controlled crowning, mediolateral episiotomy (MLE ≥60°) at instrumental birth, and avoidance of fundal pressure has been shown to reduce OASI rates by up to 50% in observational and RCT evidence. The UK NHS England OASI Care Bundle reduced institutional OASI rates by 30–40% where implemented (Vousden et al. 2019).

Hands-on perineal support RCT

The clinician's hand guides the head through controlled, slow crowning; counter-pressure on the perineum. Practised as standard care in the Nordic countries.

OR 0.43 (57% reduction in OASI). Egenberg et al. Birth 2016; Daly JO UGSA 2023. NNT ~8–12 for nulliparous SVB.

Warm perineal pack (38–44°C) RCT

A pack soaked in warm water and held against the perineum during the second stage reduces tissue stiffness and improves distensibility.

OASI 4.2% vs 8.7% in control group. Dahlen et al. BMC Pregnancy Childbirth 2007. Also reduces crowning pain by ~50%.

MLE ≥60° at instrumental birth Observational

A mediolateral episiotomy at ≥60° from the midline at crowning in all nulliparous instrumental births. Midline episiotomy INCREASES OASI risk (OR 3.0) — must be avoided.

OR 0.40 (60% reduction). Okeahialam IUJ 2022. NNT ~4–6 for nulliparous instrumental birth.

Controlled crowning Observational

Asking the mother to breathe / pant rather than push during crowning, allowing the head to pass slowly and reduce the impact force on the perineum.

Combined with hands-on support, contributes to the overall bundle effect. Part of the OASI Care Bundle (Vousden et al. BJOG 2019).

Avoidance of fundal pressure Observational

Fundal pressure (Kristeller manoeuvre) significantly increases OASI risk and is no longer recommended. Associated with shoulder dystocia and fetal hypoxia.

OR 1.9–4.0 for OASI with Kristeller. FIGO 2017; Daly JO UGSA 2023. Should not be performed.

Antenatal pelvic floor physiotherapy RCT

Supervised pelvic floor muscle training reduces OASI risk and long-term UI. Recommended for all nulliparous women from 20 weeks.

RR 0.72 for UI at 12 months postpartum (Bø et al. 2014). Reduces prolapse symptoms (OR 0.63). No reduction in OASI itself, but reduces subsequent PFD severity.

Perineal massage (35+ weeks) RCT

Antenatal perineal massage from 35 weeks (4–5 min daily) reduces OASI rate in nulliparous women. Most benefit in women ≥30 years.

OR 0.82 (18% reduction in OASI). Beckmann MM & Stock OM. Cochrane 2013. NNT ~25 for nulliparous term birth. Cannot replace intrapartum bundle but is complementary.

IOL timing for macrosomia RCT

Induction at 38 weeks for EFW ≥4 kg reduces shoulder dystocia and OASI compared with expectant management. Big Baby Trial evidence (2024).

Big Baby Trial (Groom et al.) Lancet 2024: IOL at 38 weeks for EFW ≥4 kg reduced CS and shoulder dystocia. Effect on OASI: estimated RR 0.75–0.85. OASI risk lower at 38 vs 41 weeks due to reduced head circumference growth.

Prior OASI and Subsequent Pregnancy

Risk stratification and birth planning

Women with prior OASI are at elevated risk of recurrence with vaginal birth (~4–8× baseline OASI risk) and progression of anal incontinence. Risk stratification using anorectal manometry and TPUS enables individualised counselling. The goal of subsequent-pregnancy management is to prevent recurrence while respecting patient autonomy and avoiding unnecessary caesarean section.
