Pelvic Floor & Childbirth

Understanding your anatomy, how birth affects the pelvic floor, your individual risk factors, and evidence-based steps that can help protect these tissues during labour and birth.

🧬

1. Pelvic Floor Anatomy & Function

Structure, layers, connective tissue supports, and nerves

The pelvic floor is a hammock-shaped group of muscles, ligaments, and connective tissue spanning the base of the pelvis. It supports the bladder, uterus, and rectum; maintains continence; contributes to sexual function; and stabilises the trunk. During vaginal birth it faces the most extreme mechanical demand of any structure in the human body.

🔵 Levator Ani Complex

The main pelvic floor muscle. Normal resting hiatal area ~25 cm². During second stage, must expand to accommodate a fetal head of 60–80 cm² — a 3–5× increase in area.

  • Puborectalis — anorectal sling; critical for continence and defaecation control
  • Pubococcygeus — pubis to coccyx; supports pelvic organs
  • Iliococcygeus — lateral plate; most often avulsed from arcus tendineus fascia pelvis

🟠 Perineal Body

Fibromuscular node 3–5 cm long at rest (posterior fourchette to mid-anus). Key anchor point for posterior compartment. Short perineal body (<2.5 cm) increases OASI risk from 5.6% → 40% — a 7× increase. Normal mean 3.3–4.5 cm.

🟣 Anal Sphincter Complex

  • EAS (external) — striated, voluntary; ~8 mm thick anteriorly (shorter in women)
  • IAS (internal) — smooth, involuntary; provides ~85% resting anal tone
  • Injury to either → obstetric anal sphincter injury (OASI) = 3rd/4th degree tear
  • Risks: anal/faecal incontinence, urgency, reduced quality of life

⚡ Pudendal Nerve (S2–S4)

Innervates EAS, external urethral sphincter, and perineal skin. Safe stretch ratio only 1.2–1.3 before injury — far exceeded during birth (iliococcygeus SR 2.73 at birth). Temporary neuropathy is nearly universal; permanent injury contributes to long-term AI and UI.

🔗 Connective Tissue Supports

Collagen and elastin fibres govern elasticity. DeLancey's three support levels (cardinal/uterosacral, paravaginal, perineal membrane) provide apical, lateral, and distal suspension. Relaxin and progesterone soften these during pregnancy, enabling dilation but reducing structural stiffness.

💡 Pelvic Floor Functions

  • Supports bladder, uterus, bowel against abdominal pressure
  • Urinary and faecal continence at rest and during cough/exercise
  • Sexual function and orgasm
  • Trunk and sacroiliac joint stabilisation
  • At birth: must stretch ~3× resting length to allow head passage

2. How Pregnancy Changes the Pelvic Floor

Hormonal, mechanical, and structural adaptations across 40 weeks

🧪 Hormonal Softening

Relaxin, progesterone, and oestrogen remodel connective tissue from early pregnancy. Collagen crosslinks loosen, glycosaminoglycan content increases, ligaments become more compliant. Necessary for birth canal dilation — but reducing stiffness at the moment of highest mechanical demand.

⇧ Increasing Pelvic Load

The growing uterus progressively increases downward load on the pelvic floor. By term, muscles may be chronically pre-stretched, reducing force-generation capacity. Amplified in polyhydramnios, obesity, and multiple pregnancies.

💊 Oedema & Vascularity

Increased vascularity improves tissue oxygen supply but contributes to perineal oedema, particularly in late labour. Oedema reduces tissue compliance, increasing tear risk. Warm packs and positioning help counteract this.

📊 Structural MRI Changes

MRI studies show pelvic floor descent and levator hiatal enlargement even before labour. Multiparous women may have pre-existing levator defects in 10–35% from prior births. Gestational diabetes impairs connective tissue quality. All affect subsequent birth risk.

💡 The pelvic floor is resilient — but has real limits

Of the ~85% of women who give birth, only 5–10% have a clinically detected sphincter injury, and only ~20% will ever need surgery for pelvic floor problems — though 30–50% will experience some pelvic floor symptoms at some point in their lives. Women and clinicians working together can reduce risk further. (Daly, UGSA 2023)

⚙

3. What Happens to the Pelvic Floor During Birth

Forces, stretch ratios, elastic limits, and injury mechanisms

Birth is the greatest mechanical challenge the pelvic floor faces. The fetal head (area 60–80 cm²) must pass through a levator hiatus normally ~25 cm² at rest. Tissues stretch beyond 3× their non-pregnant length — far exceeding the elastic limit of striated muscle. Injury occurs when forces or stretch ratios exceed what the tissue can tolerate.

⚡ Levator Ani Avulsion

Levator ani detaches from its pubic bone insertion — the commonest major obstetric injury. 10–35% of first vaginal births. Forceps triples risk. Substantially increases prolapse risk (RR 2.3–4.0) and reduces success of prolapse repair (RR failure 1.4). (Dietz & Steensma 2006; DeLancey 2007)

🔧 Forceps Forces

Forceps apply direct compression to the levator ani and displace the fetal head beyond voluntary pushing forces. Horizontal traction when the perineum is at full stretch creates maximum posterior compartment stress. Incorrect angle magnifies damage. OASI OR 3.56–4.0; levator avulsion ~3× higher.

📍 Occiput Posterior Malposition

Persistent OP at full dilatation means a larger presenting diameter (occipitofrontal vs suboccipitobregmatic), increasing pelvic floor stretch and OASI risk (OR 2.0). More likely to require rotational instrumental birth, further amplifying risk.

⇧ Large Baby / Macrosomia

Birthweight ≥4 kg: OASI OR ~2.0. Shoulder dystocia carries OR 12.07 for sphincter injury — highest OR of any single risk factor (Packet et al. UOG 2023). Macrosomia increases levator avulsion risk independently.

⚠ Rates depend critically on how you look

Birth registries record OASI in 1–6%. Prospective studies with systematic examination find 11–27%. Endoanal USS finds 26–27% in primiparous women. 5. Risk Factors for Obstetric Pelvic Floor Trauma

Key message: injuries cannot be fully predicted from risk factors alone

🔔 Forceps and long-term anal incontinence — even without detected OASI

The ProLong study showed women who had a forceps birth had higher rates of anal incontinence at 12 years (16% vs 11%) compared with caesarean or non-forceps vaginal birth, even without documented OASI. Subclinical nerve and levator muscle injury contributes. (ProLong study; Daly UGSA 2023)

6. Prevention — The Bundle of Care

Evidence-based interventions and estimated risk reductions

✅ Better Births for Women Bundle (WHA / SCV)

  • Hands-on perineal support with gentle verbal guidance at crowning
  • Warm perineal compress (38–44°C) at onset of stretching and crowning
  • Mediolateral episiotomy at ≥60° from midline when indicated (especially nulliparous instrumental)
  • Systematic genito-anal examination on all women post-birth
  • Two-clinician grading of tears using Sultan classification

WHA: women.wcha.asn.au | SCV: safercare.vic.gov.au/better-births

👐 Hands-On Perineal SupportObservational

Dominant hand supports the perineum; non-dominant applies gentle counter-pressure to fetal head. Verbal guidance — "breathe, don't push" — at crowning. Slow controlled birth of head and shoulders following the curve of Carus.

~57% reduction in OASI risk (OR 0.43 vs hands-off). Bundle component; individual contribution varies. Daly UGSA 2023; Räisänen et al.

🌡 Warm Perineal Pack (38–44°C)RCT

Warm wet pad applied at onset of perineal stretching; most important at crowning. Changed every 15–20 min (250 mL boiling + 300 mL cold water; insulated container). Not during water birth. Reduces oedema, increases compliance, directs attention to perineum.

~50% reduction in severe crowning pain (Dahlen et al. 2007 RCT). Possible OASI reduction: 4.2% vs 8.7% — trial not powered (Type I error possible). RANZCOG/RCOG: routine recommendation for all vaginal births.

✂ MLE at ≥60° from MidlineRCT + Meta-analysis

Cut at crowning (perineum distending), 1–2 cm from posterior fourchette, at 60° from midline. Episcissors-60 improve angle adherence. Indicated for all nulliparous instrumental births; consider in any high-risk nulliparous birth.

EVA Trial (BMJ 2024, n=702): OASI halved — 3.2% vs 7.3% (RR 0.44, adjRR 0.38). NNT≈24; 3 episiotomies per OASI prevented.
Meta-analysis (Okeahialam IUJ 2022, n=703,977): nulliparous forceps OR 0.32 (68% ↓); nulliparous vacuum OR 0.51 (49% ↓).
Simulation: MLE reduces perineal body stress by up to 52.2%.

🔄 Prefer Vacuum over Forceps When SafeCohort/SR

When instrumental birth is required and clinical circumstances allow, vacuum carries lower OASI and levator avulsion risk than forceps. However, forceps has higher success rates (lower failure rate). Clinical trade-off must be made by the clinician at the time.

OASI: Forceps OR 1.83 higher than vacuum (Bahl 2024).
Levator avulsion: Replacing forceps with vacuum could reduce prolapse surgery need by >30% (Dietz et al.).

📐 Safe Forceps Technique (Carus Arc)Simulation

If forceps are the appropriate instrument: follow the curve of Carus (downward arc, NOT horizontal); avoid horizontal traction when perineum is on full stretch; apply MLE ≥60° first; stop if no descent in 3 pulls; trial in theatre for mid-cavity. Communicate throughout.

Horizontal traction maximises posterior perineal stress. Carus arc reduces force on pelvic floor structures. Correct technique reduces but does not eliminate risk.

⏳ Optimise Second Stage — Passive PhaseCohort

Prolonged active pushing (4× OASI risk). Delayed pushing (passive phase up to 3 hours with epidural) allows fetal descent without maternal effort — reducing cumulative perineal tissue stress. Avoid extending total 2nd stage beyond RANZCOG/RCOG limits.

Active pushing duration OR 0.47 per unit (protective if slower). Passive phase allows tissue recovery between contractions. Total active 2nd stage: 3 hours maximum.

5. Prevention & Risk Reduction Bundle

Better Births Bundle — Intrapartum Care Package

Presented by Daly UGSA 2023 — aligned with WHA & Safer Care Victoria Better Births Collaborative:

  1. Hands-on perineal support with gentle verbal guidance — slow controlled birth of head and shoulders
  2. Warm perineal compress 38–44°C at onset of perineal stretching; changed every 15–20 min
  3. Mediolateral episiotomy ≥60° from midline when indicated; all nulliparous instrumental births
  4. Systematic genito-anal examination on all women post-birth (including rectal exam)
  5. Two-clinician grading of perineal tears using Sultan classification

✂️ Episiotomy Key Points (Daly 2023)

  • 🔴 Midline episiotomy: OASI risk OR 3.0
  • ✅ MLE ≥60°: perineal stress ↓ up to 52.2% (simulation)
  • ✅ Episcissors-60: OASI in SVD with Epis 1% vs 6.9% SVD without (85% ↓)
  • 📐 45° cut → only 22.5° effective angle at crowning; need to cut at 60°
  • ⏰ Cut at crowning (head distending perineum) — not before
  • 💬 Informed consent; debrief after birth

6. Antenatal Counselling Framework (Daly 2023)

📋

Identify & Inform

  • Education & awareness for all women during pregnancy
  • Prioritise high-risk: IOL, previous OASI, EBW >4 kg
  • Structured risk assessment at antenatal visits
  • Provide written information (DVDs etc.)
🤝

Shared Decision-Making

  • Offer all women risk-reducing options
  • Informed consent for all interventions
  • UR-CHOICE score — individualised risk counselling
  • Document discussion and patient preferences
🛡️

Antenatal Prevention

  • Pelvic floor muscle training — Cochrane evidence (↓ OASI, ↓ UI)
  • Perineal massage from 34+ weeks (↓ 3rd/4th degree tears in primips)
  • Optimise BMI; treat constipation
  • Identify perineal anatomy (short perineum)
