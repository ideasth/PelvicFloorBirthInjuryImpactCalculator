Understanding Your Pelvic Floor Risk

This tool presents evidence-based rates of birth-related pelvic floor injury and longer-term pelvic floor conditions, to help you and your clinician have an informed conversation about your birth options.

🤖 What This App Provides

  • Population rates of obstetric pelvic floor trauma (OASI, levator injury) by mode of birth — spontaneous vaginal, vacuum, forceps, caesarean section
  • Longer-term pelvic floor conditions: urinary leakage, anal leakage, pelvic organ prolapse, and sexual concerns
  • Your individualised risk based on your own pregnancy and birth factors, compared to the population
  • What can reduce risk: which factors are fixed, and which you or your care team can act on
  • National and state-level data from AIHW 2023, Victorian PSPI 2022, and published research

📚 How To Use This Tool

  • Step 1 – Tell us what matters to you in the preferences section below
  • Step 2 – See how risks are presented in the "How Risks Are Presented" tab, including what "common", "uncommon" and "rare" mean
  • Step 3 – View population rates in the "Population Rates" tab for all modes of birth
  • Step 4 – Calculate your personal risk in "My Risk Calculator" using your own details
  • Step 5 – Learn more in the Education tab about anatomy, birth mechanics, and prevention

⚠️ Important Context

  • No birth option is without risk — this tool presents risks across all modes of birth so you can compare them
  • The risks shown are population averages — your personal risk may be higher or lower
  • Many women give birth without significant pelvic floor injury, and most pelvic floor conditions can be treated
  • This tool is for education and shared decision-making — it does not replace individual clinical advice from your care team

How We Present Risks: The PANDA Approach

Research shows that people understand health risks much better when they are presented using specific principles. This app follows the PANDA framework used in evidence-based risk communication.

P Personalised

Your risk is compared to the population average. We show whether your risk is increased, similar to, or lower than the general population, based on your personal factors (age, BMI, previous birth history, baby's estimated weight, etc.).

A Absolute numbers

We present absolute risks (e.g. "3 in every 100 women") rather than relative risks alone. Relative risks (e.g. "doubles the risk") can mislead — we always show both the baseline and the modified risk so you can judge the actual difference. For example: "Forceps increases OASI risk — baseline 3%, with forceps 12%: that is 12 in every 100 women with forceps vs 3 in every 100 with spontaneous birth."

N Natural frequencies & Numeracy support

We use natural frequencies ("3 in 100") not just percentages ("3%"), because research shows natural frequencies are understood more accurately. We also provide person icon grids (100-person grids, or 1,000-person grids for rarer risks) so you can see affected vs unaffected people visually.

D Decision aids — both positive and negative framing

For every risk we show who IS affected and who IS NOT. For example: "97 in every 100 women do not have an OASI" as well as "3 in every 100 do." This balanced framing prevents overestimation of risk. Icon grids show both the green (affected) and grey (unaffected) people.

A Anchored in context

All risks are contextualised with an everyday analogy and compared across modes of birth. We also identify whether the factor causing a risk is Non-modifiable in pregnancy (e.g. your height, prior injury), Modifiable by you (e.g. pelvic floor exercises, perineal massage), or Modifiable by your care team (e.g. mode of birth, episiotomy technique, instrumental choice).

How Risks Are Ordered and Labelled

1

Ordered from highest to lowest risk

Risks are always presented from highest to lowest, so the most likely outcomes appear first. This helps you focus your conversation with your clinician.

2

Baseline population risk shown first

For each outcome, we first show the population average risk: Very common (>1:10), Common (≥1:100 – <1:10), Uncommon (≥1:1,000 – <1:100), Rare (≥1:10,000 – <1:1,000), Very rare (<1:10,000).

3

Your risk compared to population

Your personalised risk (from the calculator) is shown as: Increased, Similar, or Lower compared to the population average.

4

Factors shown with before/after risk

When a factor increases or decreases a risk, we show the baseline risk without the factor and the modified risk with it. For example:
Without hands-on perineal support: OASI risk ~6%  →  With hands-on perineal support: OASI risk ~3% (OR 0.43).

5

Modifiability labelled on every factor

Each risk factor is labelled: Non-modifiable in pregnancy — cannot be changed now (e.g. BMI, gestational diabetes already present, prior sphincter injury)  |  Patient action — you can act on this (e.g. perineal massage, pelvic floor exercises, timing of IOL consent)  |  Clinician action — your care team can act with your consent (e.g. instrument choice, episiotomy, bundle of care).

6

Vacuum and forceps shown separately

Vacuum (ventouse) and forceps are shown as separate modes of birth throughout this app, because their pelvic floor risk profiles are meaningfully different. Forceps carries higher OASI and levator avulsion risk than vacuum; vacuum has a higher failure rate. Both instruments have specific indications and your clinician's expertise and the clinical situation determine which is appropriate.

Levator Ani Avulsion by Mode of Birth

⚠️ What is levator avulsion?

Levator avulsion is detachment of the puborectalis muscle from its insertion on the pubic bone. It occurs at birth and substantially increases the lifetime risk of pelvic organ prolapse (OR 7.3) and stress urinary incontinence. It is diagnosed by ultrasound or MRI and is often asymptomatic initially. It affects 10–35% of women after first vaginal birth and is strongly associated with forceps delivery.

Sources: Dietz & Steensma 2006; DeLancey et al. 2007; Daly UGSA 2023; Deniz et al. Acta Obstet Gynecol Scand 2026.

🕐 IOL at 38–39 Weeks for Macrosomia

The Big Baby Trial (Pasupathy et al. 2023) showed that induction of labour at 38 weeks for suspected macrosomia (EFW ≥4 kg on ultrasound):

  • Reduced caesarean section rate
  • Reduced shoulder dystocia risk (without increasing harm)
  • Did not increase instrumental delivery rate
  • Did not change OASI rate significantly in this trial
  • Reduced mean birthweight at delivery
DeLancey capacity-demand framework: Pelvic floor capacity is fixed; demand (fetal head size) is modifiable. IOL may reduce demand on the pelvic floor by limiting further fetal growth.

Sources: Pasupathy et al. NEJM 2023 (Big Baby Trial); Boulvain Cochrane 2016; Daly UGSA 2023

⚠️ Limitations of EFW

Ultrasound EFW has a margin of error of ±10–15%. A baby estimated at 4,000 g may weigh 3,400–4,600 g at birth. Customised growth charts (Gestation Related Optimal Weight — GROW) and Hadlock formula estimates should both be considered. Discuss the findings with your clinician — EFW is one of several factors used to recommend IOL for macrosomia.

Pelvic Floor & Childbirth

Understanding your anatomy, how birth affects the pelvic floor, your individual risk factors, and evidence-based steps that can help protect these tissues during labour and birth.

🧬

1. Pelvic Floor Anatomy & Function

Structure, layers, connective tissue supports, and nerves

The pelvic floor is a hammock-shaped group of muscles, ligaments, and connective tissue spanning the base of the pelvis. It supports the bladder, uterus, and rectum; maintains continence; contributes to sexual function; and stabilises the trunk. During vaginal birth it faces the most extreme mechanical demand of any structure in the human body.

Urogenital Triangle

  • Bulbospongiosus muscle
  • Vagina and urethra
  • Perineal membrane (separating superficial and deep)
  • Deep extension of ischioanal fossa

Anal Triangle

  • External anal sphincter (EAS)
  • Internal anal sphincter (IAS)
  • Anus and rectum
  • Ischioanal fat

Perineal Body

Normal length: 3.3–4.5 cm. If <2.5 cm: OASI risk 40% vs 5.6% (OR 11). Assessment of perineal body length is a useful clinical tool.

Source: Daly UGSA 2023

Pelvic Floor Resilience

Of the 85% of women who have children: only 5–10% have sphincter injuries; only 20% have an operation for prolapse or incontinence, though 30–50% have symptoms. Women and clinicians can act to prevent and minimise trauma. Sphincter injuries can be effectively treated if detected and repaired well.

Source: Daly UGSA 2023

Risk Factor Paradox (Bharucha et al. 2014)

Only 57.7% of OASI cases have a modifiable risk factor. Only 9.4% of women with a modifiable risk factor had OASI. Therefore: not possible to prevent all OASI — preventive techniques should be offered to ALL women regardless of individual risk factors.

6. Prevention — The Bundle of Care

Evidence-based interventions and estimated risk reduction

✅ Better Births for Women Bundle (WHA / SCV)

  • Hands-on perineal support with gentle verbal guidance at crowning
  • Warm perineal compress 38–44°C at onset of stretching and crowning
  • Mediolateral episiotomy at 60° from midline when indicated (especially nulliparous instrumental)
  • Systematic genito-anal examination on all women post-birth
  • Two-clinician grading of tears using Sultan classification

WHA: women.wcha.asn.au | SCV: safercare.vic.gov.au

👐 Hands-On Perineal SupportObservational

Dominant hand supports the perineum; non-dominant applies gentle counter-pressure to fetal head. Verbal guidance: "breathe, don't push" at crowning. Slow controlled birth of head and shoulders following the curve of Carus.

57% reduction in OASI risk — OR 0.43 vs hands-off. Daly UGSA 2023; Risnen et al.

🌡️ Warm Perineal Pack 38–44°CRCT

Warm wet pad applied at onset of perineal stretching. 250 mL boiling + 300 mL cold water; change every 15–20 min. Not during water birth. Reduces oedema, increases tissue compliance.

50% reduction in severe crowning pain — Dahlen et al. 2007 RCT. Possible OASI reduction (4.2% vs 8.7% — trial not powered for this endpoint). RANZCOG/RCOG routine recommendation.

✂️ MLE at 60° from MidlineRCT + Meta-analysis

Cut at crowning (perineum distending), 1–2 cm from posterior fourchette, at 60° from midline. Episcissors-60 improve angle adherence. Indicated: all nulliparous instrumental births; consider in high-risk nulliparous labours.

EVA Trial BMJ 2024, n=702: OASI halved 3.2% vs 7.3% (RR 0.44). NNT 24.
Meta-analysis Okeahialam IUJ 2022, n=703,977: nulliparous forceps OR 0.32 (68% ↓); nulliparous vacuum OR 0.51 (49% ↓).

🍴 Prefer Vacuum Over Forceps (When Safe)Cohort SR

When instrumental birth is required and circumstances allow, vacuum carries lower OASI and levator avulsion risk than forceps. However, forceps has higher success rate. Clinical trade-off must be made by clinician at time.

OASI: Forceps OR 1.83 higher than vacuum (Bahl 2024). Levator: replacing forceps with vacuum could reduce prolapse surgery need by >30% (Dietz et al.).

🔹 Perineal Massage from 34 WeeksCochrane SR

Regular perineal massage in the third trimester (from 34 weeks). Reduces risk of severe perineal trauma including 3rd/4th degree tears in primiparous women. Also associated with higher chance of intact perineum at birth.

Aquino et al. SR: significantly lower risk of severe perineal trauma. Cochrane: benefits confirmed in primips. Daly UGSA 2023; Deniz et al. AOGS 2026.

🏃️ Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (PFMT)Multiple RCTs, Cochrane

Structured PFMT during pregnancy significantly reduces urinary incontinence and severe perineal trauma. High-quality RCTs and meta-analyses confirm benefit without adverse effects. Start as early as possible in pregnancy.

Cochrane SR: PFMT in pregnancy reduces UI risk and severe trauma. No relevant adverse effects. Daly UGSA 2023; Deniz et al. AOGS 2026.

3. Prevention Bundle

Better Births Bundle — Intrapartum Care Package

Daly UGSA 2023; WHA & Safer Care Victoria Better Births Collaborative

  1. Hands-on perineal support with gentle verbal guidance — slow controlled birth of head and shoulders
  2. Warm perineal compress 38–44°C at onset of perineal stretching — changed every 15–20 min
  3. Mediolateral episiotomy 60° from midline when indicated — all nulliparous instrumental births
  4. Systematic genito-anal examination on all women post-birth including rectal exam
  5. Two-clinician grading of perineal tears using Sultan classification
