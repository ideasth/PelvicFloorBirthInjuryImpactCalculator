Understanding your pelvic floor risks in childbirth

This patient decision aid helps you and your care provider explore how different birth options may affect your pelvic floor, now and in the future. It is based on the best available evidence and is designed to support — not replace — a conversation with your midwife or doctor.

Risks this tool covers
  • Anal sphincter injury (3rd/4th degree tear)
  • Levator ani (pelvic muscle) injury
  • Urinary incontinence (leaking urine)
  • Bowel/anal incontinence
  • Pelvic organ prolapse
  • Sexual dysfunction after birth
What you can explore
  • Population-level rates (national & Victorian)
  • Your personalised risk from your details
  • How risk changes with mode of birth
  • Factors that increase or lower your risk
  • What interventions can help
  • Risks at 38 vs 41 weeks' gestation
What this tool does NOT do
  • Tell you what to decide
  • Replace advice from your clinician
  • Account for all individual factors
  • Cover risks to the baby (see your clinician)
  • Provide a diagnosis
📋 Balanced information statement (IPDAS/NICE ECD8)

This tool presents both the benefits and potential harms of each birth option. No single mode of birth is risk-free. Caesarean section reduces some pelvic floor risks but carries its own surgical risks (bleeding, infection, placenta problems in future pregnancies) and risks to the baby. All information is presented in a balanced way so you can weigh what matters most to you.

How risk is communicated in this app

We follow the RCOG (Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists) and PANDA framework for presenting risk in a way that is honest, clear and easy to understand.

The PANDA approach

PANDA stands for: Personalised · Absolute numbers · Numeracy-supported visuals · Decision aid · Avoidance of relative risk in isolation.

✓ We use natural frequencies

"5 out of every 100 women" — not "5%" or "a doubled risk". Natural frequencies are understood far better by everyone, regardless of numeracy level.

✓ We show positive AND negative frames

For every risk we say who IS affected AND who is NOT affected — e.g. "5 out of 100 affected; 95 out of 100 not affected." This reduces framing bias.

✓ We use icon arrays (person grids)

A grid of 100 person icons — coloured ones show how many are affected. Research shows this improves understanding, especially for low numeracy. (Garcia-Retamero & Galesic, 2010)

RCOG risk categories

This app uses the official RCOG five-band risk language. Every risk is labelled with one of these categories.

Baseline population risk vs your personalised risk
Population (baseline) risk

This is the risk for all women in Australia of similar circumstances, based on national (AIHW) and Victorian (PSPI) data. It is shown as a grey bar and grey icon array.

Your personalised risk

Adjusted for your age, BMI, parity, baby's estimated weight, previous injuries, and labour factors. Shown as a teal/colour bar and highlighted icon array.

↑ Increased Your risk is meaningfully higher than the population average ≈ Similar Your risk is within the typical population range ↓ Lower Your risk is meaningfully below the population average
How risks are ordered and labelled
Order of presentation

Risks are always shown from highest to lowest (your personalised risk, not the population rate). This ensures the most important risk is seen first.

Modifiability labels

Every risk factor is labelled with whether it can be changed:

⬛ Not modifiable this pregnancy 🔵 You can act on this 🟢 Clinician can act on this

Examples: BMI is not modifiable during pregnancy; perineal massage is modifiable by you; episiotomy technique is modifiable by the clinician.

How factors that increase or lower risk are shown

For each factor, we show the baseline risk without the factor, and the modified risk with the factor — so you can see the actual difference it makes.

Important note on relative risk

Relative risk statements like "doubles the risk" can be misleading on their own — doubling a 1% risk gives 2%, not 50%. This app always pairs any relative comparison with the absolute numbers.

Induction of Labour (IOL) and pelvic floor risk

Deciding about IOL involves balancing risks to you and your baby at different gestations. This tab focuses on how gestation and IOL affect pelvic floor injury risk.

How gestation affects pelvic floor risk

Later gestation is associated with larger fetal size, increasing risk of shoulder dystocia (4× OASI risk) and OASI. However, IOL before 41 weeks reduces some of these risks but introduces its own considerations.

Estimated fetal weight (EFW) and macrosomia

EFW at 36 weeks (Hadlock formula) is used to estimate macrosomia risk. Birth weight >4 kg carries OR 2.0 for OASI (Daly 2023 UGSA).

Limitations
  • Risk estimates use a multiplicative odds ratio model — this may overestimate compounded risks when multiple factors are present simultaneously.
  • Base rates are drawn from population-level datasets (AIHW, Victorian PSPI); individual institutional rates may differ significantly.
  • This tool has not undergone prospective clinical validation in a randomised or observational study.
  • Levator avulsion rates are based on ultrasound studies, not registry data — registry ascertainment is markedly lower.
  • Long-term outcome data (UI, AI, POP) reflect observational cohort studies with variable follow-up.
  • Fetal weight estimation (Hadlock) has ±15% error at term — macrosomia prediction is imprecise.
  • Risks to the baby are not covered in this version.

📦 D3 Icon Array — GitHub Pages vs VPS

The icon arrays in this app are built directly using D3 v7 (CDN: cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/d3@7) — no npm package installation is needed. The arrays render SVG person icons using pure D3 data binding.

If you wish to use the d3-iconarray npm package specifically, you have two options:

  1. GitHub Pages (recommended for this app): Use the CDN build from esm.sh — add <script type="module">import iconArray from 'https://esm.sh/d3-iconarray';</script> to your HTML. No build step required. Push to GitHub and it works on Pages.
  2. VPS (if you want a local build): On your VPS (main.thinhalo.com), run: npm install d3-iconarray then bundle with vite or esbuild. Deploy the dist/ folder to Caddy at /opt/pelvic-floor/. This adds a build step but gives local control.

Verdict: For a static GitHub Pages site, the CDN approach (Option 1) is simpler and sufficient. You do NOT need the VPS to run this app. Use the VPS only if you need server-side features (patient data storage, authentication, API calls).
