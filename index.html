Understanding your risk of pelvic floor trauma and long-term dysfunction

Population-based rates of acute obstetric pelvic floor trauma and long-term pelvic floor disorders, stratified by mode of birth and Robson group. Designed to support informed shared decision-making during antenatal counselling.

Data sources: AIHW 2023, Victorian PSPI 2022
Risk tool: UR-CHOICE adapted
Gestation: 38 & 41 weeks

Clinical Disclaimer

This tool presents population-level epidemiological data and predictive model estimates to support antenatal counselling and shared decision-making. It does not replace individualised clinical assessment. All rates are approximate and sourced from Australian (AIHW 2023, Victorian PSPI 2022) and international peer-reviewed literature. Risks vary significantly by individual patient factors, clinician skill, and service setting. Always interpret with a qualified clinician.

Individual risk factors

Recognised prelabour and intrapartum risk factors for obstetric pelvic floor trauma and long-term dysfunction. Odds ratios from peer-reviewed meta-analyses.

Pre-labour risk factors
  • Nulliparity (first vaginal birth)
    Highest overall risk group; levator injury rate 10–19% at SVB vs ~5% multipara
  • Maternal age >35 at first birth
    Significantly increased risk of levator avulsion (OR ↑); less tissue elasticity
  • Short maternal height (<160 cm) + fetal weight >4 kg
    Combined effect — small capacity, large demand (capacity-demand model)
  • High BMI (>30 kg/m²)
    POP risk OR 1.75 (95% CI 1.17–2.62); UI risk elevated at all ages
  • Estimated fetal weight >4000g (macrosomia)
    Head circumference >35.5 cm: OR 3.3 for levator injury. AIHW: 12–17% of term births >4000g
  • Previous OASI — stratified by findings
    Asymptomatic, normal manometry, no residual defect: vaginal birth reasonable. EAS defect + reduced squeeze pressure (<20 mmHg): elective CS recommended (Thakar & Sultan 2025)
  • Pre-existing urinary or faecal incontinence in pregnancy
    UR-CHOICE factor U; increases post-delivery PFD risk significantly
  • Family history of PFD
    Polygenic risk scores for POP comparable to clinical risk factors (Pujol-Gualdo et al, Nat Commun 2022)
  • Gestational diabetes / macrosomia
    Increased fetal size increases demand on levator ani. Custom growth charts vs Hadlock methods differ in detection rate.
Intrapartum risk factors
  • Forceps delivery (any type)
    OR 5.9 for levator avulsion vs spontaneous birth. Highest single modifiable risk.
  • Persistent occiput posterior at full dilatation
    OR 3.9 for levator injury; larger presenting diameter. Manual rotation reduces risk.
  • Prolonged second stage of labour
    Independent risk factor for levator avulsion, OASI and urinary incontinence
  • Vacuum delivery
    OR ~1.5–2.5 for levator injury vs SVB. Significantly less than forceps. Preferred over forceps for pelvic floor protection.
  • Epidural analgesia
    Associated with prolonged second stage and increased OVD rates; indirect OASI risk factor
  • Protective: Bundle of care (OASI care bundle)
    4 elements: antenatal info, manual perineal protection, mediolateral episiotomy at 60° when indicated, systematic post-birth exam. OASI rate reduced from 3.3% to 3.0% (UK multicenter, Deniz et al 2026)
  • Protective: Warm compresses & perineal massage
    Warm compresses: RR 0.46 for OASI. Perineal massage: RR 0.49 for severe perineal trauma.
  • Protective: Vacuum preferred over forceps when OVD needed
    Levator avulsion 18% vs 49% (forceps vs vacuum; Handa et al 2015). Policy: offer vacuum first for uncomplicated OVD.
  • Onset of labour: spontaneous vs induced
    Induced labour: CS rate ~23.6%; spontaneous: ~15.3% (AIHW 2023). Induction at 39 wks may reduce injury via smaller head.

    Prevention & treatment

    Evidence-based interventions for pelvic floor trauma prevention and long-term dysfunction management.

    Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT)
    Grade A evidence. Reduces UI risk in pregnancy/postpartum (Cochrane). Post-partum PFMT reduces UI odds by 37%, POP by 56%. Refer to physiotherapy from 2–6 weeks post-birth.
    OASI care bundle (intrapartum)
    4 elements: antenatal education, manual perineal protection, mediolateral episiotomy at 60° when indicated, systematic post-birth perineal exam. Reduces OASI rate significantly (RCOG/RCM).
    Antenatal perineal massage (>34 weeks)
    RR 0.49 for severe perineal trauma. Reduces 3rd/4th degree tears and episiotomy. Recommended from 34 weeks gestation, self-administered or partner-administered daily for 5–10 minutes.
    Postpartum pelvic floor clinic
    Multidisciplinary team (urogynaecologist, physio, nurse). Early identification (within 2–6 weeks). Referral priority for: difficult birth, definite symptoms (pain, incontinence, pressure), OASI repair, levator avulsion.
    Conservative management (UI/POP)
    Bladder training, pelvic floor physiotherapy (59–80% symptom improvement), vaginal pessary (for POP), weight loss (5% reduction → ~50% decrease in UI episodes), topical oestrogen post-menopause.
    Surgical treatment
    Mid-urethral sling (SUI), colposuspension (cystocele), sacrocolpopexy (vault prolapse), OASI repair (immediate). ~20% of women require pelvic surgery in lifetime. Failure rates higher with levator avulsion present.
